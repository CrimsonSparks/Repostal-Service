Upgrade to paid

When Carl Bernstein who was a part of the famous and infamous duet in uncovering the Watergate Scandal in 1977, left Washington Post, he applied himself for six months to analyze the relations between the CIA and the journalists during the Cold War.

In an article which appeared in Rolling Stone magazine on 20th October 1977, it was claimed that more than 400 U.S. journalists were performing secret assignments for the CIA in the past 25 years. Given that the journalists met and were in close contact with people other civil servants did not, they offered their ‘help’ in ‘recruiting and handshaking’ with the foreign citizens who used to be their ‘channels’ for the secret information/ intelligence directed towards the U.S. intelligence services.’

The communication was carried out in the same way as with the CIA employees. ‘If for instance a journalist is based in Austria, he or she would normally be under the management of ‘the station chief’ in Vienna and he/ she would inform the employee on the case. Some of them, particularly the reporters from the U.S. who would often travel abroad, would directly inform the CIA officials in Langley, Virginia.’

The CIA informed ‘their own journalists’ and they would ‘help’ as well. As one CIA operative described: ‘For instance, the reporter from Vienna would tell ‘our man:’ ‘I met an interesting Vice Secretary in the Czech Republic’. We would say: ‘Could you meet him? And after you meet him/ her, could you possibly analyse him/her? And then, could you get him/her in touch with us—would you agree to us using your flat for that? The use of journalists was most comprehensive in the Western Europe, (the greatest focus was there where there was a looming threat,’ a CIA official said), Latin America and the Far East.

In the 1950s and 1960s they would transfer money to the members of the Christian-Democratic Party of Italy and the Social Democrats in Germany, who received millions of dollars from the CIA. We had our reporters all over Berlin and Vienna only to monitor ‘who the hell was coming from the East and what they were up to’, a CIA official explained.

The term ‘reporting’ for the CIA was rather broad and ‘illusive’. We would ask them ’Would you do us a favour?’ a high CIA official said.  We do understand you would be in Yugoslavia.  Have they paved all the streets yet? Where did you see all the airplanes? Were there any signs of military presence? How many Soviets did you see? If you happen to meet any Soviets, pronounce their names out loud and write them down the way it should be written. … Could you possibly arrange a meeting? Or pass on a message?’

The journalists got their fair share in their role when Salvador Allende was to be assassinated in Chile (1970-1973): they used to provide funds for Allende’s opponents and they wrote propaganda against Allende for the publications owned by the CIA which were distributed  in Chile during the Chilean offensive…the CIA generated the dubious and vague propaganda which was transmitted via the wire service from Santiago, it often appeared in the U.S. publications.’

Among the executives and owners which ‘gave away their generous cooperation’ to the agency were William S. Paley  from Columbia Broadcasting System, Henry Luce from Time Inc., Arthur Hays  Sulzberger from New York Times, Barry Bingham Sr from Louisville  Courier Journal and James  Copley from Copley News Service.  Other organizations which cooperated with the CIA include RCA Radio Corporation of America, Associated Press, United Press International, Reuters, Hearst  Newspapers, Scripps-Howard, Newsweek, Mutual Broadcasting System, Miami Herald and  the  old Saturday Evening Post and New York Herald Tribune.

The CIA officials claimed that by far the most valuable of these companies were New York Times, CBS and Time Inc.’ One CIA high official said that New York Times provided cover for ten CIA operatives between 1950 and 1966; he did not know who they were nor who ‘arranged’ it in the management.’

The most favorite way of protection for the free press was an innocent story of how the CIA perfidiously infiltrated among the reporters’ editorial offices’, which of course was a sweet tasting, sugar coated lie.

The leading U.S. publishers and editors-in chief took over the intelligence jobs ‘ We did not go around begging some poor reporters, for God’s sake’ William Colby exclaimed in the direction of the Church Committee investigators (the most thorough and  comprehensive investigation, which was led by Senator Frank Church in 1976). We would go straight to the management. They were witty.’ Some of the key persons in the media, among them Arthur Hays Sulzberger from New York Times—signed non-disclosure agreements.’ But the relations among the CIA officials and the media executives were normally friendly.’

A dozen famous columnists and TV commentators had relations with the CIA; they were called ‘the well-known assets’ in the agency circles so that they can be relied on for the secret assignments, they were regarded as receptive for the views of the Agency on different topics’. Three most read columnists appeared in New York Herald Tribune, Saturday Evening Post and Newsweek.

According to a CIA high official, young Sulzberger benefited from it in some way. He signed an NDA agreement because we gave him confidential information… We shared information, there was some ‘give and take’ indeed. We would say: ‘Hey, they would like to know this if we tell you this, will this help you act ‘in this way and that way’? Because of his access to Europe, he had an ‘open sesame’ case of sorts. We would ask him to just report.’ What was it and what did he say, how he looked, was he healthy? He was very impatient, he liked to cooperate.’

On one occasion according to what a number of CIA officials told, Sulzberger was given informative work for the Agency which almost literally could have been read between the lines of the text by the columnists in Times. My relations were completely informal—I had a lot of friends. I am sure they regard me as an asset.
